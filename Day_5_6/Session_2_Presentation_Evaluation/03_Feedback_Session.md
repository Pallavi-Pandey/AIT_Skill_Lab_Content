# Feedback Session

> **Learning Goal:** Learn how to give specific, constructive feedback, how to receive it without defensiveness, and how to convert feedback into concrete next steps.

---

## How the Feedback Session Works

After all capstone presentations are complete, the program enters a structured 30-minute feedback debrief. This session is not a critique — it is a collaborative learning exercise where every team learns from the experiences of every other team.

**Session flow:**

| Phase | Duration | What Happens |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| **Jury Summary** | 10 min | The lead jury member shares overall observations — what patterns were strong across teams, what common gaps appeared. No team is named specifically at this stage. |
| **Peer Feedback Exchange** | 10 min | Each team receives written peer feedback forms from the team that reviewed them during the Lab session. Teams read, discuss, and identify the most actionable point. |
| **Open Floor** | 5 min | Any team may ask the jury a question about their own evaluation or about data analytics practice in general. |
| **Action Item Commitment** | 5 min | Every team writes down one specific improvement they will make in the next 30 days based on the feedback received. |

## How to Receive Feedback

Receiving critical feedback is a professional skill. Here is how to do it well:

**Do:**
- Listen without interrupting. Even if you disagree, wait until the speaker finishes.
- Take notes. You will not remember the details after the session ends.
- Ask clarifying questions: "When you say the insight wasn't deep enough, can you give an example of what deeper would look like?"
- Say "thank you" — genuinely. Specific feedback is a gift.
- Separate the feedback from your identity. The jury is commenting on the work, not on you as a person.

**Do not:**
- Explain why you made the choices you made (this is defensiveness, not dialogue).
- Dismiss feedback with "that was intentional" without genuinely considering it.
- Compare your team's score to another team's score publicly.
- Sulk or disengage. A professional receives tough feedback and uses it.

**The 24-hour rule:** If feedback feels unfair in the moment, wait 24 hours before responding or reacting. Most criticism feels different after sleeping on it.

## How to Give Constructive Feedback

Good feedback is specific, balanced, and forward-looking. It is not about being kind or being harsh — it is about being useful.

**The SBI Framework (Situation → Behavior → Impact):**

| Component | What to Say | Example |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| **Situation** | The specific moment or slide | "During the Q&A, when the jury asked about missing values..." |
| **Behavior** | The observable action, not the character | "...the response was vague and referred to 'standard cleaning practices'..." |
| **Impact** | Why this matters | "...which made it unclear whether the team had actually validated the data quality." |

**Full feedback example:**
> "During the Q&A, when the jury asked about missing values, the response was vague and referred to 'standard cleaning practices.' This made it unclear whether the team had actually validated the data quality. Specifically naming the column, the percentage missing, and the imputation method chosen would have answered the question directly and built more credibility."

This is specific, behavioral, and actionable — the presenter knows exactly what to say differently next time.

## Common Feedback Categories and What They Mean

| Feedback You Might Receive | What It Is Really Saying | How to Act on It |
|---------------------------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| "Your insight wasn't deep enough." | You described the chart instead of the business implication. | Practice the "So What?" test. For every finding, force yourself to write the business consequence and the recommended action. |
| "The dashboard was hard to navigate." | The audience did not know where to look or what to do with the slicers. | Add a brief "How to use this dashboard" text box on the first page. Label slicers clearly. |
| "The recommendation was too vague." | "We recommend improving operations" is not actionable. | Recommendations must be specific: who does what, by when, with what expected result. |
| "The team seemed uneven." | One person dominated and others were not prepared. | In future projects, each member should own a defined output they can speak to independently. |
| "Great story, but I couldn't find the numbers." | The presentation was engaging but lacked data references. | Every claim must be tied to a visual or metric. Say "As you can see in this chart..." explicitly. |

## Quick Check Questions

1. A jury member says your insight "wasn't actionable." Using the What/So What/Now What framework, rewrite the following weak insight into an actionable one: "Weekend sales are higher than weekday sales."
2. Why is it counterproductive to explain your design decisions immediately when someone gives you critical feedback?
3. What is the difference between feedback and criticism? How does specificity change the impact?
